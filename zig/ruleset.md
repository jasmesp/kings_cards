# Long Live the Next King

A political card game of favor, betrayal, taxation, rebellion, and temporary monarchy.

## 1. Overview

**Long Live the Next King** is a 5-player game played with one standard 52-card deck and two fair six-sided dice.

Each round, one player serves as **King** while the other four players act as **Subjects**. Subjects compete to curry favor with the King by donating valuable cards, intercepting rival donations, forming alliances, hoarding power, and occasionally rebelling against tyranny.

Each player eventually becomes King. A player's main score is earned during their own reign, based on the total value of cards donated to them, plus any value they bring into the throne through their **Royal Coffers**.

## 2. Components

- 5 players
- 1 standard 52-card deck, no jokers
- 2 fair six-sided dice
- Paper or digital score tracker

## 3. Card Values

Cards have the following donation values:

| Card | Value |
|---|---:|
| 2–10 | Face value |
| Jack | 11 |
| Queen | 12 |
| King | 13 |
| Ace | 14 |

Suits have no default value unless a variant rule adds one.

## 4. Core Principle

This game uses ordinary five-card draw poker as its foundation for dealing, hands, deck integrity, private hands, and fair play.

Players may only deviate from that foundation when these rules explicitly permit it.

Anything not explicitly permitted by the rules is invalid.

## 5. Setup

1. Choose a random player to be the first King.
2. Determine the King order clockwise from that player.
3. Shuffle the deck.
4. Deal 5 cards face down to each player.
5. Place the remaining deck face down in the center as the **Draw Deck**.
6. Create a face-up **Discard Pile** beside it.

Each player keeps their hand secret unless a rule requires or allows them to reveal cards.

## 6. Game Structure

The game is played over a **Cycle** of 5 reigns.

During each reign:

- One player is King.
- The other four players are Subjects.
- Subjects take turns attempting to donate cards to the King.
- Other Subjects may challenge donations through **Parlay**.
- The King may adjudicate disputes, levy taxes, and manage the court.
- At the end of the reign, the King scores.

After all five players have served as King once, the Cycle ends.

For a short game, play one Cycle. For a full game, play two or three Cycles.

## 7. Winning the Game

At the end of the final Cycle, the player with the highest total score wins.

A player's score comes mainly from:

1. The total value of cards successfully donated to them while they are King.
2. A Royal Coffers bonus, if applicable.
3. Any variant bonuses agreed before play.

## 8. The King

The King does not take normal Subject turns during their reign.

The King may:

- Receive donations.
- Accept public petitions.
- Resolve rules disputes.
- Levy taxes for dishonorable conduct.
- Influence the court through speech, promises, threats, favoritism, and judgment.

The King may not:

- Secretly alter cards.
- Draw from the deck unless a rule permits it.
- Take cards from players unless a rule permits it.
- Change the written rules mid-reign.
- Invent new legal actions.

The King is politically powerful, but mechanically bound by the rules.

## 9. Subject Turns

Subjects take turns clockwise, skipping the King.

On a Subject's turn, they must choose one action:

1. **Donate** one card to the King.
2. **Draw** one card from the Draw Deck.
3. **Petition** the King.
4. **Declare Rebellion**, if conditions allow.
5. **Pass**.

After a Subject completes their action, play passes clockwise to the next Subject.

## 10. Donations

To donate, a Subject chooses one card from their hand and places it face down before the King.

The donating Subject may say anything they like about the card, truthfully or falsely.

Before the King accepts the donation, each other Subject, in clockwise order, may choose to **Parlay** to intercept or contest it.

If nobody Parlays, the donation succeeds. The card is placed face down in the King's **Favor Pile**.

The King may inspect donated cards in their Favor Pile at any time.

Subjects may not inspect the King's Favor Pile unless a rule permits it.

## 11. Parlay

A **Parlay** is a challenge to a donation.

A Subject may Parlay when another Subject attempts to donate a card to the King.

The challenger is called the **Interceptor**.

The original donor is called the **Defender**.

Only one Parlay may occur per donation. If multiple players wish to Parlay, the first clockwise eligible player after the donor has priority.

## 12. Parlay Procedure

1. The Interceptor rolls one die.
2. The Defender then rolls one die.
3. If the Defender's die is higher than the Interceptor's die, the Defender may cancel the Parlay by discarding one card from their hand.
   - If they do, the donation succeeds immediately.
   - If they do not, continue.
4. Add both dice together. This total is the **Bust Point**.
5. The Interceptor chooses one card from their hand and plays it either face down or face up.
6. The Defender chooses one card from their hand and plays it face down.
7. Reveal any face-down Parlay cards.
8. Compare each card's value against the Bust Point.

## 13. Parlay Results

Each Parlay card is either:

- **Under**: its value is less than or equal to the Bust Point.
- **Over**: its value is greater than the Bust Point.

Resolve as follows:

### One Over, One Under

The player who went Over wins the Parlay.

If the Interceptor wins, the donation is intercepted. The donated card goes to the Interceptor's hand.

If the Defender wins, the donation succeeds. The donated card goes to the King's Favor Pile.

Both Parlay cards are discarded.

### Both Over

Both players bust.

The attempted donation and both Parlay cards are discarded.

The turn ends.

### Both Under

The Parlay is inconclusive.

Both players return their Parlay cards to their hands, then each draws one card from the Draw Deck.

The attempted donation returns to the Defender's hand.

The turn ends.

### Tie on Card Value

If both cards have the same value, both cards are discarded and the attempted donation returns to the Defender's hand.

The turn ends.

## 14. Face-Up Parlay Cards

The Interceptor may play their Parlay card face up.

This has no automatic mechanical bonus.

However, a face-up Parlay card is a public signal. It may be used to demonstrate loyalty, invite alliance, bait betrayal, or establish reputation.

Players are encouraged to remember public signals.

The King may consider face-up Parlay conduct when judging petitions or taxes.

## 15. Drawing Cards

When a player draws, they take the top card of the Draw Deck into their hand.

If the Draw Deck is empty, shuffle the Discard Pile to create a new Draw Deck.

Players may never draw from the Discard Pile unless a rule explicitly permits it.

## 16. Petitioning the King

On their turn, a Subject may Petition.

A Petition is a public request for the King to act.

Common Petitions include:

- Accusing a player of dishonorable conduct.
- Asking the King to levy a tax.
- Asking the King to recognize an alliance.
- Asking for mercy after a betrayal.
- Calling attention to suspicious play.
- Demanding judgment on a dispute.

Petitions are political speech. They do not force the King to act.

## 17. Dishonor and Taxation

The King may levy a **Tax** against a Subject for dishonorable conduct.

Dishonorable conduct may include:

- Repeated betrayal.
- Bad-faith rules arguments.
- Breaking public promises.
- Abusive table behavior.
- Obvious anti-game conduct.
- Hoarding power while pretending loyalty.
- Undermining the court too aggressively.

A Tax forces the taxed player to discard one card from their hand.

The taxed player chooses which card to discard.

A King may levy at most one Tax per full round of Subject turns.

Taxes go to the Discard Pile, not to the King.

The King may not tax themself.

## 18. Tyranny

A King is considered **Tyrannical** if at least three Subjects publicly accuse the King of unjust rule during the same reign.

Examples of Tyranny include:

- Excessive taxation.
- Open favoritism without political justification.
- Ignoring clear rules.
- Punishing players for lawful actions.
- Protecting a known betrayer.
- Refusing to adjudicate disputes in good faith.

Tyranny has no automatic effect unless it triggers Rebellion.

## 19. Rebellion

If three or more Subjects accuse the King of Tyranny, any Subject may use their turn to Declare Rebellion.

Once Rebellion is declared:

- Rebels may no longer donate to the current King.
- Rebels may still Parlay.
- Rebels may still Draw.
- Rebels may still Petition.
- Rebels may openly coordinate.
- Successful Interceptors during Rebellion may keep intercepted cards or give them to a declared Rebel Leader.

The King remains King until the reign ends.

Rebellion is political, not automatic victory.

## 20. Rebel Leader

When Rebellion begins, the Rebels may publicly name one Subject as **Rebel Leader**.

The Rebel Leader has no special authority during the current reign.

However, Rebels may voluntarily give cards to the Rebel Leader after successful Parlay interceptions.

Cards given to the Rebel Leader go into that player's hand.

This allows Rebels to build a future Royal Coffers strategy.

## 21. End of a Reign

A reign ends after each Subject has taken three turns, unless players agree before the game to use a different reign length.

At the end of the reign:

1. Reveal the King's Favor Pile.
2. Add the values of all cards in the Favor Pile.
3. Add any Royal Coffers bonus.
4. Record the King's score for that reign.
5. Discard the King's Favor Pile.
6. The next player in King order becomes King.

Players keep their hands between reigns.

## 22. Royal Coffers

When a player becomes King, they carry their current hand into the throne.

Before their first moment as King, they must choose one of two options:

1. **Bank the Hand**
2. **Open the Hand**

## 23. Bank the Hand

If the King chooses to Bank the Hand:

1. Reveal their hand.
2. Add 50% of the hand's total card value to their King score for this reign.
3. Round down.
4. Discard the banked hand.
5. Draw a new hand of 5 cards after their reign ends.

Example:

A new King banks cards worth 38 total value.

50% of 38 is 19.

The King adds 19 points to their reign score.

## 24. Open the Hand

If the King chooses to Open the Hand:

1. Reveal their hand face up.
2. The next player in King order may draw any number of cards from it.
3. For each card drawn this way, the next player must discard one card from their own hand.
4. Any cards not taken are discarded.
5. The current King begins their reign with no hand.

This represents succession bargaining, patronage, and political inheritance.

The next King may use this opportunity to prepare for their own reign.

## 25. The King's Lack of Hand

While serving as King, a player normally has no active hand unless a rule grants them one.

The King does not make normal Subject plays.

After their reign ends, if the King has no hand, they draw a new 5-card hand.

## 26. Alliances

Players may make alliances, promises, threats, bargains, and deals at any time.

Deals are not mechanically binding unless a rule explicitly says so.

Betrayal is legal.

Reputation matters.

Players are encouraged to remember:

- Who donated generously.
- Who intercepted whom.
- Who played face up.
- Who betrayed an alliance.
- Who supported a tyrant.
- Who helped a rebellion.
- Who abused the throne.

## 27. Table Talk

Table talk is allowed.

Players may lie about:

- Their hand.
- Their intentions.
- Their alliances.
- Their future plans.
- The value of face-down donations.

Players may not lie about:

- The written rules.
- Public card history.
- Dice results.
- Revealed cards.
- Current score.
- Number of cards in their hand.

## 28. Legal Game Objects

Only cards from the single standard 52-card deck used at setup are legal game objects.

A card may move between zones only through an explicit legal action.

Game zones are:

- Draw Deck
- Discard Pile
- Player hands
- King's Favor Pile
- Revealed Parlay area
- Royal Coffers / Open Hand area

Cards outside these zones do not exist for gameplay purposes.

Physical possession of a card does not determine ownership or legality.

## 29. Legal Actions

Players may only perform actions explicitly permitted by these rules.

The main legal actions are:

- Donate
- Draw
- Parlay
- Petition
- Tax
- Declare Rebellion
- Give intercepted cards to Rebel Leader during Rebellion
- Bank the Hand
- Open the Hand
- Pass

Any attempted action not listed or clearly permitted has no effect.

## 30. Running Out of Cards

If the Draw Deck is empty, shuffle the Discard Pile to form a new Draw Deck.

If both the Draw Deck and Discard Pile are empty, players cannot draw until cards are discarded.

## 31. Scoring Example

Alice is King.

During Alice's reign, she receives donations worth:

- 10
- Queen = 12
- 7
- Ace = 14

Her Favor Pile total is 43.

When Alice became King, she banked a hand worth 32.

Her Royal Coffers bonus is 16.

Alice scores:

43 + 16 = 59 points.

## 32. End of Game

After the agreed number of Cycles, each player totals their King scores.

Highest score wins.

If tied, the tied player with the highest single reign score wins.

If still tied, the tied players share victory or play one final Kingless Parlay duel.

## 33. Optional Final Parlay Duel

Each tied player draws 5 cards.

Each tied player secretly selects one card.

Roll both dice to set the Bust Point.

Reveal selected cards.

Closest Over beats Under.

If all players are Under, highest Under wins.

If all players are Over, lowest Over wins.

If still tied, repeat.

## 34. Design Notes

This game is meant to create table politics.

The rules intentionally allow:

- Betrayal
- Reputation
- Coalition-building
- Tyranny
- Rebellion
- Public shaming
- Strategic generosity
- Opportunistic loyalty

The King should be powerful enough to tempt abuse, but not powerful enough to escape future consequences.

Subjects should feel weak individually, but dangerous collectively.

The best games should produce stories.
